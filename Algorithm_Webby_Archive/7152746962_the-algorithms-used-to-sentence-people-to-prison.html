Algorithmic fate
01.18.19
Hello Algorithm readers,

It can be hard to grasp the impacts of AI if your most frequent brush with machine-learning algorithms is through Facebook’s newsfeed or Google’s search rankings. But at the Data for Black Lives conference last weekend, technologists, legal experts, and community activists snapped things into perspective with a discussion on America’s criminal legal system. There, an algorithm can and will determine the trajectory of your life.

The US imprisons more people than anywhere else in the world. At the end of 2016, nearly 2.2 million adults were being held in prisons or jails and an additional 4.5 million were in other correctional facilities. Put another way, 1 in 38 Americans were under some form of correctional supervision. The scale is a nightmare, and it’s one of the few issues that still unites politicians on both sides of the aisle.

Under the immense pressure to reduce prison numbers without risking a rise in crime, courtrooms across the US have turned to automated tools in attempts to shuffle defendants through the criminal legal system as efficiently and safely as possible. This is where the AI part of our story begins.

Police departments use predictive policing algorithms to strategize where to staff their ranks. Law enforcement agencies use facial recognition systems to accelerate the arresting process. Each of these practices have garnered well-deserved scrutiny for whether they in fact improve safety or simply perpetuate existing inequities. Researchers and civil rights advocates, for example, have repeatedly demonstrated how facial recognition systems can fail spectacularly particularly on dark-skinned individuals, such as mistaking members of Congress as convicted criminals.

But the most controversial tool by far comes after police have made an arrest. Say hello to criminal risk assessment algorithms.

Risk assessment tools are designed to do one thing: take in the details of a defendant's profile, and spit out a recidivism score—the single number that estimates the likelihood that he or she will re-offend. A judge then factors that score into a myriad of decisions: for evaluating defendants’ rehabilitation needs, determining whether they should be held in jail before trial, and deciding the severity of their sentences. A low score paves the way for a kinder fate. A high score does precisely the opposite.

The logic for using such algorithmic tools goes, if you can accurately predict criminal behavior, you can better allocate resources accordingly, whether for rehabilitation or for prison sentences. In theory, it also leads to less bias in the process because judges are making decisions on data-driven recommendations and not their gut.

You may have already spotted the problem. Modern-day risk assessment tools are often built upon machine-learning algorithms that are trained on historical crime data.

As we’ve covered before, machine-learning algorithms use statistics to find patterns in data. So, if you feed it historical crime data, it will pick out the patterns associated with crime. But those patterns are statistical correlations—nowhere near the same as causations. If it found, for example, that low-income were correlated with high recidivism, it would leave you none the wiser about whether low-income actually caused crime. But this is precisely what risk assessment tools do: they turn correlative insights into causal scoring mechanisms.

Now populations that were historically disproportionately targeted by law enforcement—especially low-income and minority communities—are at risk of being slapped with high recidivism scores. As a result, the algorithm could amplify and perpetuate the embedded biases of historical criminal records and generate even more data in the process to feed a vicious cycle. Because most risk assessment algorithms are proprietary, it’s also impossible to interrogate their decisions or hold them accountable.

The debate for these tools is still raging on. Last July, more than 100 civil rights and community-based organizations, including the ACLU and NAACP, signed a statement urging to stop the use of risk-assessment algorithms. At the same time, more and more jurisdictions and states, including California, have turned to them in a Hail Mary effort to fix their overburdened jails and prisons.

Data-driven risk assessment is a way to sanitize and legitimize oppressive systems, Marbre Stahly Butts, executive director of Law For Black Lives, said onstage at the conference, which was hosted at the MIT Media Lab. It is a way to draw attention away from the actual problems affecting low-income and minority communities, like defunded schools and low access to healthcare.

“We are not risks,” she said. “We are needs.”

Research

Sing your feelings. Music streaming services like Spotify are trying a new kind of music classification. Instead of grouping songs by genre, they’re doing so by mood. You can play stations that help you focus or others that set the tone for a romantic evening. This has created a new challenge for machine-learning researchers: how do you accurately classify the emotions of songs?

Researchers at the Italy-based AI Labs and Musixmatch, a lyric-sharing platform, created a new dataset with the audio and transcribed lyrics of various songs to figure out which techniques generate the most accurate classification results. They tested different kinds of neural networks to classify the audio and the latest natural language processing techniques (NLP) to classify the lyrics—including Google’s BERT, which made headlines last year for its groundbreaking results in machine comprehension. Each technique was first trained on a batch of songs that had already been labeled by a human team with the correct emotions.

Through their experiments, the researchers determined that, of all the methods, running just the vocals from each song's audio through a neural network produced the most accurate classification of emotions. BERT, sadly, did not perform well, which goes to show that as much as NLP techniques have improved, machines still struggle to comprehend human language.
