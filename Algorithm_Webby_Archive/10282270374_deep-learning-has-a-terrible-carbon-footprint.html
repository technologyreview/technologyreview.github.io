This week I published an exclusive report on the carbon footprint of deep learning. In a new paper, researchers at the University of Massachusetts, Amherst, performed a life cycle assessment for training several common large AI models. They found that the process, which runs on energy-intensive computer chips, can emit more than 626,000 pounds of carbon dioxide equivalent—nearly five times the lifetime emissions of the average American car (and that includes manufacture of the car itself).

This is an extreme case, but the carbon footprint of a typical model is not much better. The researchers found that the process of building and testing a model required training 4,789 models over a six-month period, cumulatively emitting around 78,000 pounds of CO2 equivalent—seven times the annual footprint of the average human.

The stark numbers confirm something that AI researchers have suspected for a long time. “While probably many of us have thought of this in an abstract, vague level, the figures really show the magnitude of the problem,” says Carlos Gómez-Rodríguez, a computer scientist at the University of A Coruña in Spain, who was not involved in the research. More news

Amazon brings machine learning to fashion, again. This week at re:MARS 2019, Amazon’s AI and robotics conference, the tech giant announced several new services and features. Among them, a tool called StyleSnap promises to supercharge your search for a perfect outfit. You use it by uploading photos and screenshots of clothes and accessories you like. It then uses machine-learning algorithms to match them to similar items on Amazon.

The tool is the company’s latest crack at one of the few areas it has yet to dominate in retail. Last year, it took a different tack with the launch of a stylist assistant built into the Echo Look camera. But reviewers found the assistant’s tips lacking, and the product never took off. This time, Amazon may have more luck. With StyleSnap, it’s leaning back on the strengths of machine learning: pattern-matching in images. Read more here.

Beijing weighs in on AI ethics. Last Saturday, Chinese scientists and engineers released an AI code of ethics that might signal a willingness from Beijing to rethink how it uses the technology. Called the Beijing AI Principles, the code spells out guidelines for research and development in AI, including that “human privacy, dignity, freedom, autonomy, and rights should be sufficiently respected.” While it would be easy to dismiss talk of privacy and individual freedoms as disingenuous, it signals a surprising willingness to discuss such issues within Chinese policy circles.

The move comes at a critical moment for China-US relations. Alarmed by China’s progress in areas like AI and 5G, the Trump administration has used the levers of global trade to attack and, in some cases, cripple key Chinese tech firms. The US government is also said to be considering export controls that will target Chinese companies that sell surveillance equipment and software, such as Hikvision and Dahua Technology, because of their role in helping the Chinese government implement surveillance. In that climate, finding common ground may prove difficult, but it makes the Beijing Principles all the more important. Read more here.

The guy who made a porn actress identifier is sorry. Last week, an anonymous programmer based in Germany caused outrage for supposedly using face-recognition technology to “catch” women who had appeared in porn. There is still no proof that the global system—which allegedly matched women’s social-media photos with images from sites like Pornhub—actually worked, or even existed. Still, the technology exists to carry it out, and it would have had awful consequences.

The programmer says he’s since deleted the project and all its data, but that’s not an act of altruism. Such a project would have violated European privacy law anyway. And if the programmer had charged money to access this information, he could have faced up to three years in prison under German criminal law. But not everyone has those same protections, and the programmer isn’t the only one able to build this technology. The episode shows how important it is for policymakers to think ahead as face recognition grows ever more pervasive. Read more here.

The risks of AI-generated text. Deep-learning techniques have made it easier and easier for anyone to forge convincing misinformation. But just how easy? Two researchers at the United Nations decided to find out. In a new paper, they used only open-source tools and data to show how quickly they could get a fake UN speech generator up and running.

They used a readily available language model that had been pre-trained on text from Wikipedia and fine-tuned it on all the speeches given by political leaders at the UN General Assembly from 1970 to 2015. Thirteen hours and $7.80 later (spent on cloud computing resources), their model was spitting out realistic speeches on a wide variety of sensitive and high-stakes topics from nuclear disarmament to refugees.

The case study demonstrates the speed and ease with which it’s now possible to disseminate fake news, generate hate speech, and impersonate high-profile figures with disturbing implications. The researchers conclude that there needs to be a greater global effort to work on ways to detect and respond to AI-generated content. Read more here.

The lion, the fish, and the robot. Lionfish are invasive predators that are threatening native fish and crustaceans in the Atlantic Ocean. A submersible robot, called Guardian LF1, is on a mission to hunt, stun, and capture them. It features eight thrusters, an onboard computer, a camera, and a power source, along with a set of low-voltage “stunning panels” and a chamber for storing captured fish. It is controlled from the surface using a tether but includes an autopilot and a computer vision system capable of distinguishing lionfish from other species. The robot costs around $1,000 but could net its operator $1,500 to $2,000 worth of lionfish on a good day, its creators claim (assuming the fish can be sold for food). Read more here.
